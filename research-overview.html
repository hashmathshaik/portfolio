Research Direction

My research centers on reliable reasoning in large language models — building systems that don't just generate plausible-sounding output, but can be verified, constrained, and trusted in open-ended, multi-step tasks. I approach this from a neuro-symbolic angle: pairing LLMs with symbolic structure (knowledge graphs, concept networks, operational and axiomatic semantics) so that generation is checked against explicit constraints rather than relying on the model's fluency alone.

A second thread of my work is agentic collaboration — using generator–validator agent pipelines and tool-augmented workflows to let multiple LLM-based agents cross-check each other's reasoning in collaborative coding and open-ended problem-solving settings. The goal in both threads is the same: reduce hallucinated or unsupported reasoning steps and make LLM outputs auditable, whether the task is validating a team's problem-solving process, generating circuit topologies, or extracting information from real-world documents.

A third, increasingly central direction is mechanistic interpretability for improving reasoning — looking inside models to understand the internal computations behind their reasoning, and using those insights to make reasoning more reliable rather than treating the model as a black box. This complements the neuro-symbolic and agentic threads: where those add external structure and verification, interpretability aims to understand and steer the reasoning process from within.

This research direction has carried directly into applied work at Amazon Science, where I've worked on graph representation learning for fraud detection and multimodal document understanding — both settings where reliability, not just accuracy, is the binding constraint on deployment.
